# Curricular Proposal — v2 (Strengthened)

## Reinforcing Physical Education and *Vida Saludable* in Mexican Lower-Secondary Education: A Mechanism-First, Environment-Anchored Intervention

> A complement to the Nueva Escuela Mexicana (NEM) — Plan de Estudios 2022 — for students aged 12–15.
> This version supersedes `curricular_proposal.md`. It is the design submitted for the five expert-validation interviews.

---

## 0. What changed from v1, and why

v1 was a sound *policy* draft but carried three weaknesses that the school-health literature punishes hard. v2 confronts each one explicitly:

| Weakness in v1 | What the evidence says | What v2 does |
|----------------|------------------------|--------------|
| Implicit causal chain *knowledge → behaviour → BMI* | Nutrition knowledge is a weak-to-null predictor of adolescent eating behaviour; large RCTs (e.g. WAVES, BMJ 2018; HEALTHY, NEJM 2010) changed knowledge yet barely moved adiposity | We make the theory of change explicit, ground it in three behavioural theories, and add an **environmental/structural component** because education alone reliably fails |
| BMI framed as the primary outcome | BMI in adolescents is noisy, slow-moving, underpowered at pilot scale, and ethically fraught (weight stigma) | We demote BMI to a **secondary/exploratory** outcome and elevate **behavioural and environmental** outcomes that the intervention can actually move, plus a **formal mediation test** of the mechanism |
| Underpowered parallel pilot (3 schools/state) | School-clustered effects on adiposity are tiny; cluster designs lose power to the ICC | We switch to a **stepped-wedge cluster-randomised design**, add an explicit **power analysis with ICC assumptions**, and **pre-register** |

The intellectual centre of gravity also moves: the original contribution is no longer "an education module" but **the Mexican *tiendita* (nanostore) as the obesogenic node, and the school as a citizen-science instrument that measures and acts on it**.

## 1. Context and Justification

### 1.1 What already exists

The NEM Plan 2022 includes Educación Física as a subject (currently 2 h/week in secondary) and *Vida Saludable* as an articulating axis (eje articulador) within the campo formativo *De lo humano y lo comunitario* — i.e. a transversal theme, not a dedicated subject. In practice PE has been compressed from up to 5 h/week (pre-2010) to 2 h/week, and *Vida Saludable* depends on each school's interpretation: no standardised content, no teacher-training package, no dedicated assessment. SEP statements (2024–2025) acknowledge the gap (banning ultraprocessed food from schools, promoting water) but classroom-level reinforcement remains weak.

> **Verify before sharing:** confirm the current PE hour allocation and the exact 2024–2025 SEP measures against the primary Plan 2022 / Programa Sintético and the official SEP bulletins. These figures anchor the justification.

### 1.2 Why complement, not replace

A new subject requires federal curricular reform — a politically improbable timeline. Reinforcing existing slots is faster, cheaper, operationally realistic, and respects the work already invested in the NEM framework. This is a *feasibility* argument, and it is genuinely strong; it does not need to be oversold.

### 1.3 Why lower-secondary (12–15)

| Criterion | Evidence | Note |
|-----------|----------|------|
| Higher prevalence | Adolescents 12–19: 40.1 % overweight + obesity vs 36.6 % in 5–11 (ENSANUT 2020–2024) | *Verify exact figures and survey wave* |
| Discretionary spending | Secondary students manage their own money and visit nanostores autonomously | This is the behavioural lever the design exploits |
| Habit consolidation | Adolescent habits track into adulthood (Singh et al., 2008) | Tracking ≠ causation; state as plausibility, not proof |
| Cognitive readiness | Capable of critical analysis of their food environment | Enables the audit-as-intervention design |
| Access for validation | Director of Sec. Téc. 78 is among the five validators | Feasibility check built in |

## 2. Theoretical Framework (NEW — the spine v1 lacked)

The intervention is designed *backwards from a mechanism*, not forwards from content. Three complementary theories specify how change is supposed to happen:

1. **Socioecological model (McLeroy et al., 1988).** Adolescent eating is determined far more by environment (what is cheap, salient, and within arm's reach at the nanostore) than by individual knowledge. Therefore an education-only intervention is mis-specified. We intervene at *two* levels: the individual (literacy + autonomy) **and** the proximal environment (point-of-decision at nanostores, school food policy linkage).

2. **Self-Determination Theory (Ryan & Deci, 2000).** Adolescent behaviour change is sustained by *autonomy* and *competence*, not by instruction or prohibition. Lecturing teenagers about nutrition predictably backfires. The audit/citizen-science component is designed to be **autonomy-supportive**: students investigate *their own* environment and reach *their own* conclusions, which the SDT literature predicts will produce more durable internalisation than didactic delivery.

3. **Dual-process / behavioural economics (present bias, salience, default effects).** The nanostore purchase is a fast, System-1, point-of-sale decision driven by salience and price, not a deliberative one. Knowledge delivered in a classroom on Tuesday does not reach the moment of purchase on Friday. The structural component (below) therefore targets the **decision environment itself** — the moment and place where the choice is actually made.

**Design implication:** any component that only adds knowledge is, by this framework, expected to fail in isolation. Every knowledge component in v2 is paired with either an autonomy-supportive practice or an environmental change.

## 3. Theory of Change (explicit logic model)

```
INPUTS            ACTIVITIES                 PROXIMAL OUTCOMES         INTERMEDIATE              DISTAL (exploratory)
(what we put in)  (what students do)         (weeks)                   (months)                  (1+ year)

Teacher CPD   →   Active-Lifestyle Literacy → Functional label/       → Self-reported           → Adiposity (BMI z-score)
Resource pack     (3rd PE hour)               budgeting literacy ↑       purchasing shift ↑         [SECONDARY, may be null]
                                                                         (validated FFQ-lite)
Nanostore     →   Environment audits        → Critical food-           → Observed nanostore      → Sustained behaviour
audit kit         (CBPR / citizen science)    environment perception     basket change at          (maintenance, RE-AIM)
                                               ↑; sense of agency ↑       partner stores ↑
Point-of-     →   Default & salience nudges → Healthier default       → School-level food
decision kit      at partner nanostores       chosen at point of sale    access map improves
                  + school food-policy link

Family bridge →   Monthly take-home         → Family conversation      → Home food-environment
                  (invitation, not homework)  frequency ↑                shift (self-reported)
```

**The mechanism we are testing:** the intervention works *through* changing the proximal food environment and adolescent agency over it — **not** through knowledge per se. This is falsifiable. If purchasing behaviour shifts without a knowledge shift, our mechanism is supported over the v1 model; if knowledge rises but behaviour does not, we have replicated the field's null and must say so.

## 4. Proposal Components

### 4.1 Component A — Extended Physical Education (3 h/week, up from 2)

- **Session 1 (60 min):** Traditional physical activity — sport, motor skills, team play (existing).
- **Session 2 (60 min):** Traditional physical activity — conditioning, fitness fundamentals (existing).
- **Session 3 (60 min) — NEW:** *Active-Lifestyle Literacy*, with a structured cognitive component, **always paired with an applied task** (never knowledge-only).

| Week of cycle | Topic | Applied / autonomy-supportive task |
|---------------|-------|-------------------------------------|
| 1 | Reading nutrition labels | Interpret a label on a *real* product brought from a nanostore |
| 2 | Healthy snack on a 20-peso budget | Design, cost, and (cycle 2+) actually buy and rate it |
| 3 | Mapping the food environment | Field exercise feeding the Component B dataset |
| 4 | Cooking demo, simple ingredients | Practical literacy (guest cook where available) |
| 5 | Heart-rate, effort, recovery | Self-measurement; links activity to measurable feedback |
| 6 | Sleep, hydration, screen time | Self-tracked one-week log |
| 7 | Marketing literacy | Deconstruct a real ad targeting their age group |
| 8 | Reflection + family-conversation prep | Transfers learning home (feeds Component D) |

Cycles repeat with deeper content. *Verify with directors that the third hour is schedulable; fallback in §9.*

### 4.2 Component B — *Vida Saludable* Critical-Environment Module (the original-contribution engine)

Embedded in any subject touching *Vida Saludable* (Geografía, Formación Cívica, Ciencias Naturales). Every two weeks (30 min within an existing class) students audit the food environment around the school using a **standardised audit kit** (so the data are comparable across schools):

- Count and classify products in 3 nearby nanostores against a fixed taxonomy.
- Record prices of healthy vs ultraprocessed options.
- Geolocate access points; build a cumulative school food-access map.
- Present findings to other grades / parents at a year-end assembly.

**Why this is the contribution, not the garnish.** The audit is simultaneously (a) an *autonomy-supportive intervention* (SDT), (b) a *community-based participatory research* instrument, and (c) a **data engine**: each cohort produces standardised, geolocated nanostore food-environment data that Mexico largely lacks at scale. The school becomes a research partner, and the dataset feeds the Phase-1/Phase-2 analyses. This is the part most likely to interest a research committee, because it generates *new knowledge about the obesogenic environment*, not just delivers existing knowledge to students.

### 4.3 Component C — Point-of-Decision / Structural Layer (NEW)

Education without environmental change is the documented failure mode. So we add a light structural layer at the actual point of purchase, co-designed with willing nanostore owners near partner schools:

- **Salience & default nudges:** reposition water/fruit to eye level and the front; place a low-cost "healthy snack under 20 pesos" shelf tag (designed by students in Component A).
- **School food-policy linkage:** connect the audit findings to the school's existing implementation of the 2024–2025 SEP ultraprocessed-food measures, closing the loop between *measuring* the environment and *changing* it.
- Owner participation is **voluntary and incentive-light** (signage, recognition); we measure uptake honestly and report non-participation as data.

This component is what makes the causal story plausible. It targets the System-1 moment of choice, where the classroom cannot reach.

### 4.4 Component D — Teacher Training Package

- **8 h asynchronous CPD** for PE teachers and any *Vida Saludable* deliverer: nutrition fundamentals, label literacy, NEM alignment, **autonomy-supportive pedagogy** (explicitly, so teachers don't revert to lecturing), classroom management for outdoor/field sessions, and **audit-protocol fidelity** (so the Component B data stay comparable).
- **Optional 4 h synchronous workshop** with a sport-medicine or public-health practitioner.
- **Resource pack:** printable nutrition cards, label-reading worksheets, standardised nanostore-audit templates, nudge-signage templates, a 30-item test bank.

### 4.5 Component E — Family Bridge

A monthly take-home **invitation** (not enforced homework): discuss one dimension of the month's content, try one low-cost recipe together, return a one-page reflection signed by parent and student. Designed around the dominant survey finding that families lack *time*; fidelity tracked, never punitive.

## 5. Study Design and Evaluation (the part that earns rigour)

### 5.1 Design: stepped-wedge cluster-randomised trial

Rather than a parallel pilot, schools (clusters) cross from control to intervention in randomised sequence at fixed steps. Advantages over v1's parallel design:

- **More statistical power** from within-cluster comparisons — important when between-school variance (ICC) is high.
- **Ethically and politically superior:** every participating school eventually receives the intervention, which matches how a real SEP rollout would happen and eases recruitment.
- **Built-in temporal control** for secular trends.

### 5.2 Outcomes (re-ordered from v1)

**Primary (behavioural & environmental — what the intervention can actually move):**
- Self-reported food-purchasing behaviour at nanostores (short validated food-frequency instrument).
- Observed nanostore basket / inventory change at partner stores (objective).
- Critical food-environment literacy and sense of agency (validated scale).

**Secondary / exploratory (slow, noisy — reported with honesty):**
- Adiposity (BMI z-score), explicitly flagged as likely **underpowered at pilot scale** and ethically handled (no individual labelling; aggregate only).

**Mechanistic:**
- **Formal mediation analysis**: does behaviour change run *through* environmental/agency change rather than knowledge alone? This directly tests v2's theory against v1's failed model.

### 5.3 Power analysis (must be done before the pilot, not after)

Cluster designs require accounting for the intra-cluster correlation (ICC). For adiposity outcomes in school-clustered trials, ICCs are typically small (~0.01–0.05) but inflate required sample size via the design effect `1 + (m − 1)·ICC`. Behavioural outcomes usually carry larger detectable effects than BMI, which is *another* reason to make them primary.

> **Action item:** compute the minimum detectable effect (MDE) for the stepped-wedge design at plausible ICC values and cluster sizes *before* committing to "3 schools per state." If the BMI MDE is unrealistic, state plainly that the pilot is powered for behavioural/environmental outcomes and that adiposity is exploratory. Saying this *up front* is what distinguishes a credible design from one that quietly produces an uninterpretable null.

### 5.4 Pre-registration and reporting

- Pre-register the protocol, primary/secondary outcomes, and analysis plan **before** data collection (e.g. OSF), to immunise against outcome-switching.
- Report against the appropriate CONSORT extension for cluster/stepped-wedge trials.

### 5.5 Implementation evaluation (RE-AIM)

Most school interventions fail not because the idea is wrong but because implementation collapses. We evaluate on the RE-AIM dimensions — **Reach, Effectiveness, Adoption, Implementation (fidelity), Maintenance** — with monthly fidelity checks. Fidelity-of-implementation is treated as a measured variable, not an assumption.

## 6. The Phase-5 Monte Carlo, Done Right

A Monte Carlo projection is only as honest as its priors. v2 fixes this:

- **Priors grounded in meta-analytic effect sizes** for comparable school-based interventions, **not** invented optimistic numbers. Where the meta-analytic central estimate for adiposity is near zero, the prior must reflect that.
- **Separate models per outcome:** behavioural/environmental outcomes (where effects are plausibly detectable) modelled separately from adiposity (where the credible interval should comfortably include the null).
- **Sensitivity analysis** across optimistic / central / pessimistic effect-size and ICC assumptions; report the full distribution, not a single headline number.
- **The null is a presented outcome, not a hidden one.** If the simulation shows that the most likely BMI result is indistinguishable from zero, that is the finding, and it is consistent with the literature. The behavioural case can still be strong.

This is precisely the move that turns "a simulation that looks sophisticated" into "a simulation a reviewer trusts."

## 10. Expected Outcomes

Quantitative projections come from the Phase-5 Monte Carlo (now honestly bounded). Qualitative:

- Students gain functional, *applied* food literacy (paired with action, not knowledge alone).
- Students develop measurable critical agency over their food environment.
- Partner nanostores shift defaults at the point of decision (the mechanism).
- Families gain a structured, time-respecting conversation channel.
- The SEP gains a low-cost reinforcement of existing axes with **no legislative change** and **a reusable food-environment dataset**.

## 11. Risks and Mitigation

| Risk | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|
| Teacher resistance / added workload | Paid CPD; reuse PE slot; autonomy-supportive design reduces prep burden vs lecture-writing |
| Teachers revert to lecturing (kills the mechanism) | CPD explicitly trains autonomy-supportive delivery; fidelity checks measure it |
| Nanostore owners decline to participate | Component C is voluntary; non-participation reported as data, not hidden |
| Class management in outdoor/field sessions | Optional workshop addresses directly |
| Family non-engagement | Invitation, not enforcement; tracked, not punitive |
| Underpowered BMI result misread as "it doesn't work" | BMI pre-specified as exploratory; primary outcomes are behavioural/environmental |
| The documented null long-term result | Confronted in the theory of change, the outcome hierarchy, and the Monte Carlo priors — not just acknowledged in a sentence |
| Weight-stigma harm from BMI measurement | Aggregate-only, no individual labelling, ethics-reviewed protocol |
