# Script — English Version · 15 minutes
## "From Nanostores to Classrooms" · MIT LiftLab 2026

> **Team:** Fernanda Ita [F] · Alexis Marcos [A] · Carlos Torres [C]
> **Target:** 15 minutes maximum + Q&A
> **Tone:** conversational, honest — tell the real story

---

## SLIDE 1 — TITLE · 40 sec · [F]

> "MIT gave us a question: what keeps people from buying healthy products in Mexico's nanostores?
>
> This is our answer in four acts. And we start with the failure — because that failure was exactly what led us to the right answer."

---

## SLIDE 2 — THE DATASET · 1 min · [C]

> "MIT gave us access to 3,040 real field records: shoppers interviewed at the moment of purchase, store owners, last-mile delivery workers.
>
> On top of that, we ran our own survey — 104 responses in San Luis Potosí.
>
> This is not the story of 'we used data and found the answer.' It's the story of how the data surprised us twice."

---

## SLIDE 3 — ACT 01: THE REGRESSION · 1:30 min · [C]

> "We started where every analyst starts: a linear regression. If we know a shopper's demographics and budget, we should be able to predict their purchasing behavior.
>
> The result: R² of **0.032**. The model explains almost nothing.
>
> But that wasn't a failure — it was our **first real finding**: purchasing behavior in nanostores doesn't depend on who you are. Something more structural is at play. And that's what pushed us to Act 2."

---

## SLIDE 4 — ACT 02: THE NUMBERS · 1:30 min · [C]

> "We changed the question. Instead of how much someone spends, we asked what actually gets bought and how the whole system looks.
>
> Four numbers that make you stop:
>
> **60.8%** picks up at least one unhealthy item.
>
> **50.3%** — exactly half — picks up *only* unhealthy products. Nothing healthy at all.
>
> Only **23.7%** picks up anything healthy.
>
> And **66%** of last-mile deliveries didn't even offer the store any healthy products. The problem doesn't start at the counter — it starts with what arrives at the store."

---

## SLIDE 5 — THE HEART OF THE FINDING · 2 min · [C]

> "But the number that truly changes the diagnosis is here.
>
> **85%** know that what they buy daily is bad for them. **63%** say they would buy healthier if they could. **65%** want more options available.
>
> But only **24%** actually buy anything healthy."

*[2-second pause.]*

> "That's a **39-point gap** between what people want to do and what they actually do.
>
> We also ran a Random Forest — more advanced AI — to see if we could predict who buys healthy. AUC of **0.55**. Barely better than a coin flip.
>
> The conclusion: healthy purchasing is not about who you are. The problem is structural — price, availability, what gets stocked."

---

## SLIDE 6 — ACT 03: OUR SURVEY · 1:30 min · [A]

> "To validate, we went to the field with an independent sample: 104 surveys in San Luis Potosí.
>
> The top reason people gave for not cooking: lack of time because of work.
>
> And when we asked what solutions they wanted..."

*[Pause.]*

> "Our own respondents named nutrition education among their top three.
>
> **80 out of 94 said they would change their habits** with more time or resources.
>
> The MIT dataset and our own field survey — two completely independent sources — gave us exactly the same answer."

---

## SLIDE 7 — THE BRIDGE · 1 min · [F]

> "Our data is about adults. Our intervention targets adolescents. We're honest about that gap.
>
> The bridge: if people already know and already want, but the system doesn't give them the conditions, then the highest-leverage solution is **educating the next generation of shoppers**.
>
> Why secondary school? Because at ages 12–15 they already handle their own money, visit nanostores independently, and the habits formed then consolidate into adult life.
>
> We're not regulating the nanostores. We're educating those who will visit them for life."

---

## SLIDE 8 — ACT 04: THE PROPOSAL · 1:30 min · [F]

> "Four components, all within what Mexico's National Curriculum already names — no new subject required:
>
> **A** — An extra hour of PE for active lifestyle literacy: reading labels, planning a healthy snack on a real budget.
>
> **B** — A critical environment module: students audit the nanostores around their own school.
>
> **C** — Teacher training. Without prepared teachers, nothing reaches the classroom.
>
> **D** — A monthly family bridge activity that respects the time constraints families themselves said are their main problem.
>
> Estimated cost: **MXN 9,500 per school per year**. Scaled nationally: less than 0.05% of the federal education budget."

---

## SLIDE 9 — MONTE CARLO · 1:30 min · [C]

> "To project the impact we ran a Monte Carlo simulation: 1,000 students, **5,000 runs per scenario**, with effect sizes from published meta-analyses.
>
> The central scenario — the most likely based on the literature — projects obesity in the cohort falling from **31.9% to 26.2% at 5 years**. Minus 5.7 percentage points.
>
> And here, the **null-result scenario**, where the intervention has no lasting effect. We include it intentionally — because hiding it would make us dishonest."

---

## SLIDE 10 — HONESTY · 45 sec · [C]

> "We explicitly separated what we know, what we assume, and what we don't know yet.
>
> Being honest about limitations doesn't weaken the proposal. It makes it credible."

---

## SLIDE 11 — EXPERT VALIDATION · 2 min (incl. videos) · [A]

> "We brought the proposal to five experts with completely different perspectives: Tec de Monterrey researchers, a secondary school principal in Matehuala, a PE teacher, and an education student.
>
> Independent interviews. The same themes emerging without any coordination between them."

*[Play video clips.]*

---

## SLIDE 12 — CLOSE · 45 sec · [F]

> "MIT asked: what stops healthy purchasing in nanostores?
>
> It's not ignorance. It's not lack of willpower. The system is structured to push unhealthy choices — and people don't have the conditions to act on what they already know and already want.
>
> The most effective long-term solution is not in regulating the nanostores."

*[Pause.]*

> "It's in the classrooms.
>
> Thank you."
