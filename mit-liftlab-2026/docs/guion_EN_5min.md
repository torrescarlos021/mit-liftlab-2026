## SECTION 1 — MOTIVATION, OBJECTIVES & RESEARCH QUESTION (1 min)

---

### SLIDE 1 — TITLE + RESEARCH QUESTION · 1 min · [F]

> "MIT gave us a question: what keeps people from buying healthy products in Mexico's nanostores?
>
> We had access to 3,040 real field records across four actor types — shoppers, store owners, delivery workers, and nanostore operators — plus 104 of our own survey responses from San Luis Potosí.
>
> This is not the story of 'we used data and found the answer.' It's the story of how the data surprised us, and where it led us."

*[Point to the research question on screen.]*

## SECTION 2 — METHODOLOGY, DATA COLLECTION & ANALYSIS (1.5 min)

---

### SLIDE 2 — METHODOLOGY: TWO ACTS OF DISCOVERY · 45 sec · [C]

> "We started where every analyst starts: a linear regression. Predict purchasing behavior from demographics and budget.
>
> R² of **0.032** — the model explains almost nothing. Five folds, all near zero. One even negative.
>
> But that wasn't a failure. That was our **first real finding**: behavior in nanostores doesn't depend on who you are. Something structural is at play.
>
> So we changed the question. Instead of how much someone spends, we asked what gets bought and why. We also ran a Random Forest — more advanced AI — to predict who buys healthy. AUC of **0.55**. Barely better than a coin flip.
>
> Both models are telling us the same thing: the barrier is not in the person. It's in the system."

---

### SLIDE 3 — RESULTS: THE REAL PICTURE · 45 sec · [C]

> "Four numbers that stop you:
>
> **60.8%** picks up at least one unhealthy item. **50.3%** — exactly half — picks up *only* unhealthy products. Only **23.7%** picks up anything healthy. And **66%** of last-mile deliveries didn't even offer healthy products to the store.
>
> But the number that truly changes the diagnosis is this:
>
> **85%** know what they buy is bad for them. **63%** say they would buy healthier. Only **24%** actually do.
>
> That's a **39-point gap** between what people want to do and what they actually do. The problem is not ignorance. The problem is what the system makes available to them."

---

## SECTION 3 — RESULTS, FINDINGS & CONCLUSIONS (2.5 min)

---

### SLIDE 4 — OWN SURVEY: INDEPENDENT VALIDATION · 30 sec · [A]

> "To validate, we ran our own independent survey: 104 responses in San Luis Potosí.
>
> The top barrier to healthy eating: no time because of work — 62% of respondents.
>
> And when we asked what solutions they wanted, our own respondents named nutrition education as their number one answer. The exact intervention we were already designing.
>
> Two completely independent data sources. The same diagnosis."

---

### SLIDE 5 — THE SOLUTION: CURRICULUM INTERVENTION · 45 sec · [F]

> "The bridge is this: if people already know and already want, but the system doesn't give them the conditions, then the highest-leverage solution is educating the next generation of shoppers.
>
> Why secondary school? Because at 12–15, students already handle their own money, visit nanostores independently, and the habits formed then last a lifetime.
>
> Four components, all within Mexico's National Curriculum — no new subject required:
>
> **A** — An extra PE hour for label literacy and healthy snack planning on a real budget.
> **B** — An environment audit where students map the nanostores around their own school.
> **C** — Teacher training. Without prepared teachers, nothing reaches the classroom.
> **D** — A monthly family bridge activity that respects the time constraints families named as their main problem.
>
> Estimated cost: **MXN 9,500 per school per year** — less than 0.05% of the federal education budget."

---

### SLIDE 6 — MONTE CARLO + EPISTEMIC HONESTY · 45 sec · [C]

> "To project the impact, we ran a Monte Carlo simulation: 1,000 students, **5,000 runs**, effect sizes drawn from published meta-analyses.
>
> Three scenarios at year 5:
>
> Without any intervention, obesity in the cohort rises from **31.9% to 36.4%** — the natural trend.
>
> The central estimate — most likely based on the literature — drops it to **26.2%**. Minus 5.7 percentage points.
>
> And we show the null-result scenario — where the intervention has no lasting effect — because hiding it would be dishonest.
>
> On the right you see what we know, what we assume, and what we don't know yet. In MIT's competition, we're not looking for perfect projects. We're looking for honest ones."

---

### SLIDE 7 — CLOSE · 20 sec · [F]

> "MIT asked: what stops healthy purchasing in nanostores?
>
> It's not ignorance. It's not lack of willpower. The system is structured to push unhealthy choices — and people don't have the conditions to act on what they already know and already want.
>
> The solution is not in the nanostores."

*[Pause.]*

> "It's in the classrooms.
>
> Thank you."
