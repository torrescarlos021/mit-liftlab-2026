§01 — The Problem

One of the world's most severe
childhood obesity crises.

Mexico carries a child and adolescent obesity burden that ranks among the highest globally. The crisis sits at the intersection of three structural realities: an ultraprocessed food environment, a time-scarce labour economy, and an education system whose health-curriculum is underfunded in practice.

§02 — Analytical Pipeline

Five phases, one chain of reasoning.

Our work follows a deliberate sequence: descriptive statistics first, then population data, then primary fieldwork, then intervention design, then projection. Each phase tests the assumption that the previous one rests on.

01

Linear Regression

OLS on state-level data linking nanostore density and child obesity prevalence, controlling for marginalization and urbanization.

02

Extended Datasets

Behavioural and intake variables (ENSANUT PA module, ENIGH spending, Anuarios de Morbilidad) refine the picture.

03

Primary Survey

104 respondents in SLP test the root-cause hypothesis: lack of time is the dominant structural barrier.

04

Intervention Design

Concrete reinforcement of PE and Vida Saludable, validated by five experts across research, leadership, and practice.

05

Monte-Carlo Simulation

5,000 runs project BMI trajectories at 1, 3, and 5 years under five scenarios, with effect sizes drawn from published meta-analyses.

§03 — Key Findings from the Survey

The population names
the solution itself.

Of 104 surveyed respondents, the picture is consistent: structural time scarcity drives unhealthy eating; parents are aware and concerned; and the most-requested solution is precisely the one we propose — nutrition education paired with access to healthy options.

§04 — The Intervention

Four components, built on what already exists.

Lower-secondary students (ages 12–15) are the target population — both because their obesity prevalence is the highest and because adolescent habits track strongly into adulthood. The intervention reinforces, rather than replaces, the existing NEM curriculum.

A

Expanded PE

From 2 h/week to 3 h/week. The added session is dedicated to Active Lifestyle Literacy — reading labels, planning healthy snacks on a real budget, mapping the food environment.

B

Critical Environment Module

Biweekly audits of the nanostores around the school. Students count and classify products, document prices, and present findings. Pure NEM articulating-axis territory.

C

Teacher CPD

An 8-hour asynchronous training package plus an optional 4-hour synchronous workshop. Without trained teachers, nothing works.

D

Family Bridge

A monthly take-home activity — discussion prompt, low-cost recipe, signed reflection. Not homework: an invitation that respects family time.

§05 — Monte-Carlo Simulation

Five years out, projected and honest.
