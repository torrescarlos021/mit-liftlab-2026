Section 1 Interview Protocol

Expert interviews were conducted to validate the research diagnosis, stress-test the intervention design, and surface implementation risks that desk research cannot reveal. Five independent experts were selected to represent different vantage points: academic research, school administration, classroom practice, and the student perspective.

All interviews were audio recorded with explicit informed consent from each participant. No interview was conducted anonymously — participants agreed to be identified by name and role.

Structure

Semi-structured format: 8 identical core questions asked to all five experts, with 2–3 role-specific questions designed to probe each expert's domain knowledge.

Rationale for Expert Selection

The five-person panel was designed to avoid epistemic homogeneity. Two university researchers provide methodological rigor; one school director provides ground-level institutional insight; one practicing teacher provides classroom-reality checks; one emerging educator provides a student-proximate perspective. No two experts hold identical roles, which maximizes the diagnostic value of convergence findings.

Section 3 Key Themes — Convergence Analysis

Thematic coding identified five high-signal themes. Convergence was scored using the pre-defined threshold: 4 or 5 of 5 experts = strong; 3 of 5 = converging. No themes fell below 3/5.

5/5
Theme 1 · Structural Barriers Structural barriers confirmed
✓ Strong — 5/5
All five experts agreed, independently and without prompting, that healthy behavior change requires structural enabling conditions — not only individual motivation or knowledge. This is the strongest convergence finding in the study and directly validates the core research diagnosis. No expert contested this framing.
4/5
Theme 2 · Curricular Fit Intervention aligns with NEM curriculum
✓ Strong — 4/5
Four of five experts confirmed that the proposed intervention — embedding nutrition and physical activity content within existing subjects — aligns naturally with Mexico's NEM (Nueva Escuela Mexicana) framework and does not require creating a new subject. The one dissenting expert raised implementation complexity, not philosophical disagreement.
5/5
Theme 3 · Teacher Readiness Teacher preparation is the critical bottleneck
✓ Strong — 5/5
Every expert flagged teacher preparation as the single most critical factor for intervention success. The concern was not whether teachers are willing, but whether they have the domain knowledge and pedagogical confidence to deliver nutrition and physical activity content effectively. This finding directly shaped the training component of the intervention design — teacher preparation is now a first-class deliverable, not an afterthought.
3/5
Theme 4 · Family Bridge Family component is feasible with caveats
~ Converging — 3/5
Three of five experts supported including a family engagement component to extend healthy-habit formation beyond school hours. However, all three included important caveats: frequency must be manageable for working parents (monthly, not weekly), content must be immediately practical, and the school must have infrastructure to support family communications. Two experts were neutral — not opposed, but skeptical of realistic family participation rates in SLP context.
4/5
Theme 5 · Cost Realism Budget of 9,500 MXN/school/year is plausible
✓ Strong — 4/5
Four experts agreed that the proposed cost of approximately 9,500 MXN per school per year is a realistic figure for a pilot-scale school program in SLP. This is consistent with existing per-student program budgets in the state's public secondary system. The fifth expert noted that cost projections typically underestimate training and coordination overhead in year one, a caveat incorporated into the intervention's cost structure.

Section 4 Post-Validation Design Refinements

Expert interviews are not merely validations — they are a design input. The following four changes were made to the intervention design as a direct result of expert feedback. Each change is traceable to specific interview findings.

  • A
    PE Hours / Week — Component A
    The original fixed value was replaced with a flexible parameter. Expert 03 (school director) clarified that scheduling constraints vary significantly by school — some schools have more PE time than others. The final design specifies a minimum and leaves exact weekly hours subject to adjustment based on school scheduling audits conducted during onboarding.
  • B
    Module Placement by Subject
    The assignment of specific content modules to specific subjects (e.g., nutrition science in Biology vs. Health class) was left open rather than prescribed. Expert 04 (practicing teacher) noted that subject ownership of cross-cutting content is politically sensitive in many schools. Final design: module placement is determined after a teacher training audit, not before.
  • C
    Teacher Training Format — Asynchronous First
    Original design assumed synchronous workshops as the primary training format. Experts 01 and 02 recommended asynchronous-first delivery with an optional synchronous workshop, citing scheduling conflicts typical in public secondary schools. The revised format makes asynchronous modules mandatory and synchronous cohort sessions optional but available.
  • D
    Family Bridge Frequency — Monthly Confirmed
    The originally proposed biweekly family engagement cycle was reduced to monthly. Expert 03 (school director) noted from direct experience that biweekly family sessions create administrative burden and typically see attendance drop sharply by month two. Monthly cadence, confirmed by three experts, was adopted as the pilot standard.
Design Honesty — What Changed

Expert validation did not simply confirm the original design. It changed it in four specific, traceable ways. We view this as the correct outcome of a validation process: if every expert had agreed with every original decision, that would suggest the questions were not probing enough.

All four refinements make the intervention more deployable, not more ambitious. This reflects a deliberate design philosophy: an intervention that can be reliably implemented at modest fidelity is more valuable than a theoretically optimal program that schools cannot sustain.
