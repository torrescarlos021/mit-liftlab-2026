Section 2 Phase 1 — Linear Regression
Phase 1 · OLS Regression

The first analytical phase tested whether demographic and structural variables could predict obesity outcomes across Mexican states using ordinary least-squares regression.

Model specification: OLS regression of nanostore density versus child obesity rates across all 32 Mexican states, controlling for the marginalization index and urbanization rate.

Validation strategy: 5-fold cross-validation to test generalizability. All folds returned R² values near zero; one fold returned a negative R², indicating no predictive signal.

Cross-Validation Result

All 5 folds: R² ≈ 0 or negative.
No predictive signal detected.
The model does not generalize.

R² = 0.032
Regression explains 3.2% of variance

Nanostore density, marginalization index, and urbanization rate together explain only 3.2% of the observed variation in child obesity across 32 states. This is not a statistically meaningful signal.

→ Purchasing behavior does not significantly depend on demographic predictors. The problem is structural, not individual.

Interpretation — Why This Null Result Matters

A failed regression is not a wasted analysis. It tells us that simple demographic proxies cannot explain why people make unhealthy food choices at nanostores. The mechanism is not "poor people in dense areas buy bad food." The mechanism is deeper — structural unavailability, time constraints, and broken supply chains. This null finding redirected the entire research design.

Intention–Action Gap

AUC = 0.55 — What This Means

A Random Forest classifier with AUC of 0.55 is barely above random chance (0.50). Even with expanded features including activity data, spending data, and morbidity data, the model cannot reliably predict who will make healthy purchases. This confirms that healthy purchasing behavior is driven by contextual constraints, not individual characteristics we can easily measure.

Cross-Source Convergence — Key Validation

Two independent data sources, same diagnosis: The MIT multi-actor field dataset (3,040 records) and our own SLP survey (104 respondents) converge on the same central finding: the primary barrier to healthy food behavior is structural — time, access, and supply-chain availability — not individual knowledge or motivation.

This convergence across independent methods substantially strengthens the credibility of the research diagnosis and the design of the proposed intervention.

Section 5 Statistical Validity & Honest Reporting

We report all results, including null findings. Scientific integrity requires that failures be documented as clearly as successes.

No Cherry-Picking

The regression failure (R² = 0.032) was reported as our first finding, not concealed. Null results in Phase 1 and near-chance AUC in Phase 2 are prominently disclosed.

Data & Code Availability

All analytical code and processed datasets are available upon request to competition reviewers. Raw MIT data subject to original data-sharing agreements.

Why Null Results Are Valuable

The two near-null machine learning results (R² = 0.032, AUC = 0.55) are not failures of analysis — they are findings. They rule out simple demographic and behavioral explanations for the obesity-purchasing relationship, forcing us toward the structural diagnosis that motivates the intervention design. A study that only reports positive results would have led to the wrong conclusions.
