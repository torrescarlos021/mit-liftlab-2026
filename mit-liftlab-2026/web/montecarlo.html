Section 1 Simulation Configuration

The Monte Carlo model propagates uncertainty from published effect-size confidence intervals through a projected 5-year trajectory for a cohort of secondary-school students. Each scenario runs 5,000 independent trials to obtain a stable distribution of outcomes.

Reproducibility

All simulations use random seed 42. Full code available upon request. Results are stable across re-runs — the 5,000-trial count was chosen to ensure the standard error of the mean ΔBMI estimate is below 0.02 kg/m².

Section 2 Effect-Size Parameters

Each intervention component is parameterized using effect sizes drawn from peer-reviewed meta-analyses. Confidence intervals from the original studies are used as the uncertainty bounds in the Monte Carlo sampling. A null-robustness check using a long-term null study is included as a conservative control.

Intervention Component ΔBMI (kg/m²) 95% Confidence Interval Source
Nutrition education alone −0.33 (−0.55, −0.11) Silveira et al. 2013
Physical activity alone −0.13 (−0.22, −0.04) Cochrane review
PA + nutrition combined −0.17 (−0.29, −0.06) Cochrane review
Health ed, adolescents (z-score) −0.06 (−0.10, −0.03) Brown et al. 2020
Long-term null (robustness check) 0.00 (−0.05, +0.04) Hodder et al. 2021
Null Robustness Check — Why It Is Included

Hodder et al. (2021) found no long-term BMI effect from school-based interventions. Including this in the simulation as a dedicated scenario reflects epistemic honesty about the uncertainty in long-term maintenance of effects. A null scenario that closely tracks the baseline confirms the model behaves correctly under conservative assumptions.

Section 5 Interpretation & Limitations

The Monte Carlo model is a decision-support tool for estimating the expected range of outcomes under uncertainty. It is not a prediction of what will happen — it is a structured way to ask "what range of outcomes is consistent with what we know from the literature?"

Central Finding

Under published effect-size estimates, the intervention reduces obesity prevalence by approximately 5.7 percentage points at the 5-year horizon. This is a meaningful public-health effect size for a school-based program.

  • ⚠ Projection, not prediction. This model cannot account for unmeasured local factors, policy changes, or teacher-specific implementation quality. Real-world deployment requires a controlled pilot study with measured outcomes.
  • ⚠ Effect sizes from international studies. The meta-analyses used were not conducted specifically in Mexican secondary schools. Effect sizes may differ in this population due to baseline health status, cultural diet patterns, and school infrastructure.
  • ⚠ The null scenario is intentional. Hodder et al. (2021) represents studies where long-term effects faded. Including this scenario reflects the genuine possibility that short-term BMI gains do not persist. It is not a reason to abandon the intervention — it is a reason to design for maintenance.
  • ⚠ BMI as a proxy. BMI is an imperfect measure of health. The model does not capture changes in physical fitness, dietary quality, mental health, or academic performance, all of which are plausible secondary benefits of the intervention.
Recommended Next Step

A 12-month pilot with a quasi-experimental design (treatment school + matched comparison school) would provide measured effect sizes in the target population, replacing the international estimates with locally-valid parameters and dramatically improving model accuracy.
