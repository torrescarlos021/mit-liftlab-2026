Video Testimonials Five Experts, One Converging Diagnosis

We conducted semi-structured video interviews with five independent experts — representing educational research, school administration, classroom practice, and fitness pedagogy. Their independent accounts corroborate the structural diagnosis at the heart of our research.

Validation Protocol

Each interview lasted 30–45 minutes. Participants were briefed on the research question but not on our findings, to avoid anchoring their responses. All recordings were made with informed consent. Thematic analysis applied convergence scoring: 4–5/5 experts endorsing a theme = strong; 3/5 = converging.

Interview Format
Semi-structured · 8 core questions + 2–3 role-specific
Recording
Audio-video, informed consent obtained
Analysis Method
Thematic coding: convergence, divergence, unexpected findings
Convergence Threshold
Strong ≥ 4/5 experts · Converging = 3/5

Expert #1 · Educational Research Dr. José Manuel Olais Govea
01
Dr. José Manuel Olais Govea
Tec de Monterrey, Campus SLP
Educational Research
Research Focus

Curriculum design and health promotion within formal education systems. Expert on integrating health content into the Vida Saludable axis of the NEM framework.

Key Contribution

Validated that the NEM curriculum already contains the structural hooks our intervention leverages — no new subject required. Confirmed that the Vida Saludable axis is frequently underimplemented due to insufficient teacher training.

Interview Recording — Dr. Olais Govea
~35 minutes · Recorded April 2026
Core Position

"The issue is not curriculum design — the NEM framework is actually well-structured. The gap is at the implementation layer. Teachers receive the plan but not the tools or time to execute the health components meaningfully."

"El currículo tiene los ejes. Lo que falta es la formación docente para activarlos. Si le das al maestro 8 horas de capacitación relevante, puedes cambiar completamente cómo se enseña Vida Saludable."
Dr. Olais Govea · Interview, April 2026
Impact on Intervention Design

Led to the inclusion of Component C (Teacher CPD, 8h + 4h optional) as a non-negotiable part of the intervention. Without this, Dr. Olais estimates adoption rate below 20%.

Classroom Practice
Role Context

Active secondary-school teacher with additional background in personal fitness instruction. Provides dual perspective: formal classroom experience and applied health behavior change from one-on-one coaching.

Key Contribution

Brought the practitioner lens: what activities actually work within a 50-minute PE session vs. what looks good on paper. Recommended the "healthy snack on ≤20 MXN budget" challenge as a high-engagement, low-cost activity that directly addresses the price barrier students identify.

Interview Recording — J.T. Pedraza
~28 minutes · Recorded April 2026
Core Position

"The problem is not that kids don't know what's healthy — most of them do. The problem is that when they're hungry after school, standing in front of a nanostore with 20 pesos, everything available is chips and soda. We need to teach them what to ask for, not just what to avoid."

"Les digo a mis alumnos: tienes 20 pesos, necesitas rendir toda la tarde. ¿Qué compras? La mayoría elige lo que le ofrecen porque no han practicado elegir. Eso es entrenable. Igual que un movimiento atlético."
J.T. Pedraza · Interview, April 2026
Impact on Intervention Design

The "healthy snack on a real budget" activity (Component A, active literacy session) came directly from this interview. Also informed the decision to ground nutrition education in practical scenarios, not abstract dietary guidelines.

Cross-Expert Analysis Convergence Across All Five Voices

Five independent experts from different roles — university research, school administration, classroom teaching, and student practice — converged on the same core themes without being primed to do so.

The barrier is structural, not motivational
Convergence 5/5
All five experts independently stated that the gap between intention and action at nanostores is driven by environmental and structural factors — price, availability, time pressure — not by lack of awareness or willpower. This matches both the R²=0.032 regression result and the AUC=0.55 ML finding.
Teacher training is the non-negotiable leverage point
Convergence 4/5
Four of five experts specifically called out teacher preparation as the make-or-break factor. Without CPD (Component C), all other components become unreliable. This validated the 8h+4h training investment as essential, not optional.
12–15 is the right age window
Convergence 5/5
All five agreed that early adolescence — when students begin making independent purchases and habits are forming — is the highest-leverage point for a school-based intervention. Later is less effective; earlier lacks the purchasing autonomy that makes the lesson experiential.
Family engagement must respect time constraints
Convergence 4/5
Four experts warned that family-facing components will fail if they feel like homework or add burden to working-class households. The monthly cadence and "invitation" framing of Component D came directly from this consensus.
NEM curriculum is adequate; implementation is the gap
Convergence 3/5
Three experts from the education sector confirmed that the NEM's Vida Saludable axis theoretically supports this intervention without curriculum reform — the problem is under-implementation, not policy absence. This supports the "no new subject required" design constraint.
Epistemic Honesty — Limitations of Qualitative Validation

Selection note: Experts were identified through professional networks accessible to the team. This introduces potential sampling bias toward perspectives that are sympathetic to education-based interventions. We cannot claim this sample represents the full range of expert opinion on nanostore health interventions.

Anchoring risk: Despite our protocol to withhold findings, some experts may have been influenced by the framing of our questions. Quotes and summaries above represent our interpretation of their positions and have not been member-checked.

What this validates: The expert interviews provide qualitative triangulation that the structural diagnosis is credible, the intervention design is feasible, and the implementation risks (teacher training, family engagement, modular rollout) are real and addressable. They do not replace the quantitative evidence; they complement it.
